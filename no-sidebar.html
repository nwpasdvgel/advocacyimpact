Online Advocacy

For civil society organizations, grassroots groups, and individual researchers, online tools now make meaningful participation in global decision-making far more accessible. What once required a constant physical presence at key UN locations can now be done from anywhere. Virtual meetings, online consultations, written submissions, and pre-recorded video statements allow targeted inputs without the logistical and financial barriers that previously limited access.

Many UN mechanisms actively encourage online participation. Human rights treaty bodies, Special Rapporteurs, and UN working groups regularly invite civil society to join virtual briefings or submit information through online questionnaires. These formats help deliver precise recommendations grounded in local realities. Even brief, two-minute interventions can shape discussions, highlight emerging issues, and bring attention to communities often overlooked.

Online participation is a powerful entry point for shaping global agendas and ensuring that diverse voices are heard.

Human Rights Council and NHRIs

The Human Rights Council (HRC) is the principal United Nations body responsible for promoting and protecting human rights globally, and for addressing violations where they occur. Much of the work happens long before issues reach the Council floor; by the time matters are formally presented, key advocacy and preparatory work has already taken place.

This preparatory work is conducted through the UN’s subsidiary bodies, thematic working groups, and special procedures. Special Rapporteurs, independent experts, and specialized consultation mechanisms gather information, solicit civil society input, and produce analyses that inform Council deliberations. These spaces are often more accessible and allow targeted contributions, making them essential for effective advocacy.

Role and Potential of National Human Rights Institutions (NHRIs)

NHRIs occupy a unique space between government and civil society, giving them credibility and authority. They serve as independent watchdogs, monitoring the "gap" between the human rights standards governments have committed to and the lived realities of citizens. Their work includes:

  • Public Awareness and Education: NHRIs build community understanding of human rights standards, translating complex legal concepts into everyday language that resonates with citizens.
  • Media Engagement: Under the Paris Principles, NHRIs publicize human rights and efforts to combat discrimination through all press organs, advocating for freedom of expression.
  • Systemic Monitoring: They monitor the legislature, executive, and judiciary to ensure human rights obligations are met.
  • Investigative Role: Acting as "detectives," NHRIs investigate fraud, corruption, and systemic abuses, pressuring governments to be more transparent and accountable.

Mechanisms for Accountability

NHRIs and the HRC use several formal mechanisms to fulfill their mandates:

  • Paris Principles: Define the status, independence, and functions of NHRIs, including their media engagement role.
  • Universal Periodic Review (UPR): Enables NHRIs to engage with international human rights bodies and raise awareness of states’ obligations.
  • National Inquiries: Conduct systemic investigations into violations, using public hearings and testimonies to build public records.
  • Complaint Handling: Provide channels for individuals to report violations and discrimination, ensuring formal follow-up.
  • Special Procedures and Rapporteurs: Experts such as the Special Rapporteur on freedom of expression report on emerging threats like digital surveillance and AI impacts (Irene Khan report).
  • Reporting Portals and Monitoring Frameworks: Some NHRIs partner with civil society to create online reporting tools and track digital rights compliance regionally.

Role in the Digital Age

The HRC and NHRIs increasingly focus on digital rights. Hundreds of HRC resolutions address privacy, surveillance, and digital freedoms. NHRIs now advocate for the "freedom to connect," supporting freedom of expression and assembly online, countering disinformation, collaborating with fact-checking networks, and using social media to mobilize public action.

NHRIs and the HRC act like the immune system of democracy: they detect threats, identify abuses, and trigger responses through advocacy and legal reform to keep societies healthy and rights-respecting.

Human Rights Trainings

Human rights are universal, indivisible, and inalienable. They are inherent to every individual, cannot be lawfully taken away, and are mutually reinforcing, with each right carrying equal significance. Effective human rights advocacy requires a comprehensive understanding of the context in which one operates—whether in diplomatic settings, public demonstrations, national-level engagements between governments and civil society, or international forums. Each environment demands a carefully calibrated approach, informed by local realities, the stakeholders involved, and the broader political and social landscape.

Successful advocacy also necessitates diligent attention to safety. While challenges and risks may arise, including potential reprisals, strategic engagement and rigorous risk assessments help protect both individuals and organizations. Support from mechanisms such as the Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights can further enhance these efforts and provide essential safeguards.

We support advocates in designing strategies that are effective, secure, and impactful. From refining messaging to connecting with expert advisors and activating relevant mechanisms, we enable meaningful change while navigating complex human rights spaces with confidence and professionalism.

Holding governments accountable depends on a collaborative ecosystem in which independent media, civil society organizations, human rights defenders (HRDs), and National Human Rights Institutions (NHRIs) perform mutually reinforcing roles. Journalists act as the public’s watchdog—providing the “eyes and ears” that scrutinize the legislature, executive, and judiciary—while civil society often functions as a “detective,” investigating corruption, abuse of power, and systemic wrongdoing and supplying verified evidence for sustained public-interest reporting. Increasingly, activists and community defenders rely on digital platforms to document violations, preserve evidence, and mobilize public attention, in line with emerging global standards on digital rights and participation reflected in the United Nations Global Digital Compact.

Effective accountability further requires strategic information delivery to the press. Civil society organizations enhance impact by maintaining “story banks” of documented cases and trained spokespeople, producing media-ready materials that respond to shrinking newsroom resources, and cultivating professional relationships grounded in trust and respect for journalistic standards. These practices help translate legal norms into public understanding and pressure, reinforcing jurisprudence such as the African Court on Human and Peoples’ Rights’ landmark ruling in Konaté v. Burkina Faso, which affirmed that disproportionate criminal sanctions against journalists violate freedom of expression and democratic principles.

Finally, accountability systems must protect the messengers and actively engage the public to pressure authorities. Joint advocacy networks, coordinated monitoring frameworks, and transparent reporting portals strengthen information integrity and early warning across borders, complementing regional commitments such as the Treaty for the Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa (COMESA). At the same time, NHRIs and HRDs—guided by the Paris Principles on National Human Rights Institutions—must consistently defend press freedom and challenge laws and practices that create a chilling effect on journalism. Initiatives such as UNESCO’s UN Plan of Action on the Safety of Journalists and the Issue of Impunity demonstrate that when states, media, and civil society act together, accountability becomes a cornerstone of democratic, rights-based, and environmentally sustainable governance.

Civil Society at the Forefront

Across Africa and globally, civil society organizations, journalists, and human and environmental rights defenders play a pivotal role in safeguarding democracy, advancing justice, and protecting shared resources. Their work—often undertaken at great personal risk—connects legal frameworks, international commitments, and the realities on the ground.

Key regional and global instruments underscore not only the legitimacy of civil society, but its indispensability. Together, they form a growing architecture of protection, accountability, and participation that must be actively implemented to realize rights in practice.

Digital Governance and Civic Participation

As civic engagement increasingly moves online, the United Nations Global Digital Compact seeks to shape a digital future rooted in human rights, inclusion, and accountability. Digital technologies can empower civil society—but unchecked surveillance, disinformation, and digital repression pose serious threats to defenders and journalists worldwide.

By calling for universal access, protection of human rights online, and meaningful participation, the Compact highlights civil society’s critical role in digital governance. Human rights and environmental defenders are often early adopters of digital tools for documentation, mobilization, and advocacy, making their protection in digital spaces a priority.

Success depends not on declarations alone, but on sustained engagement with civil society actors who understand both the promise and the risks of digital transformation.

Protecting Truth-Tellers

Journalists remain among the most targeted defenders of human rights and the environment. The UNESCO-led UN Plan of Action on the Safety of Journalists and the Issue of Impunity addresses this crisis by promoting prevention, protection, and prosecution.

Attacks on journalists are not isolated incidents—they are systemic failures that undermine democracy, accountability, and sustainable development. Ending impunity is essential for safeguarding the public’s right to know.

Civil society organizations play a crucial role in documenting attacks, supporting affected journalists, and pressing states to fulfill their obligations. Environmental journalists and community reporters often operate in high-risk contexts where powerful interests intersect with weak governance.

Shared Responsibility

These instruments demonstrate that civil society, journalists, and human and environmental rights defenders are central to realizing rights, justice, and sustainable development—not peripheral actors.

Legal frameworks alone are insufficient. Political will, adequate resources, and sustained international solidarity are required to ensure protections are implemented and civic space remains open.

In an era of climate crisis, digital transformation, and democratic backsliding, supporting civil society is not charity—it is a strategic investment in resilient, inclusive, and rights-based societies. The courage of defenders on the frontlines shows that progress is possible, but never automatic. It must be defended, collectively and continuously.
